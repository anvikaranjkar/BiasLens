What is gender bias in language?

It's not about intent.
It's about patterns.

Most people who use biased language don't mean to. The bias lives in what we default to, what we assume, and who we leave out — invisibly, habitually, and at scale.

01

Pronoun asymmetry

Using "he" as the default for unknown-gender subjects. Research shows this causes readers to picture male subjects even when the text is meant to be neutral.

e.g. "Each student must submit his assignment"
02

Gendered language

Words that embed a gender assumption — "chairman", "mankind", "manpower". These treat maleness as the universal default and all have neutral alternatives.

e.g. "Mankind has always sought…" → humanity
03

Anthropomorphism

Giving non-human things a gender without reason — ships as "she", AI assistants as female for service roles, weapons as "he". Each choice reinforces stereotypes.

e.g. "She set sail on her maiden voyage" — a ship
04

Domain stereotyping

Entire fields implicitly assumed to belong to one gender. STEM and finance default to male. Nursing and teaching default to female. Even without explicit pronouns.

e.g. "Ask your nurse — she'll explain" (13% of nurses are male)
Spot it yourself

the bias — and the fix

Job listing
"We need a self-made man who can put in the man-hours to lead the team."
Hover to flip →
✓ Neutral version
"We need a driven, hardworking leader for our team."
Three gendered terms: "self-made man", "man-hours". Biased listings get 42% fewer female applicants.
AI / Tech
"Our AI assistant — she learns your preferences and adapts to your needs."
Hover to flip →
✓ Neutral version
"Our AI assistant learns your preferences and adapts to your needs."
74% of AI assistants are given female voices for service roles. UNESCO flagged this globally in 2019.
Science writing
"The great men of science have always pushed the boundaries of mankind's knowledge."
Hover to flip →
✓ Neutral version
"Scientists have always pushed the boundaries of human knowledge."
"Great men" + "mankind" erases Marie Curie, Ada Lovelace, Rosalind Franklin from history.

Add your key to detect.html

Open detect.html in any text editor. Find this line near the top and replace the placeholder with your key:
