Definition

What is gender bias
in language?

"Gender bias in language is the systematic tendency to use words, phrases, or structures that reflect, reinforce, or create unequal assumptions about gender — often invisibly, often habitually, and often with real consequences for how people see themselves and others."
— Synthesised from linguistics and social psychology research

It isn't about individual intent. Most people who use biased language don't mean to. The bias lives in the patterns — in what we default to, what we assume, and what we leave out.

🔠

Lexical bias

Individual words that carry a gender charge — "mankind", "chairman", "stewardess". These words assume a default gender for a concept or role.

e.g. "man up", "manpower", "mankind" all treat maleness as the universal default

🔄

Generic masculine

Using "he" as the default pronoun for a person of unknown gender. Research shows this causes readers to picture male subjects even when the text is meant to be neutral.

e.g. "Each student must submit his assignment"

📍

Pragmatic bias

Bias embedded in how language is used in context rather than in the words themselves. What gets mentioned, in what order, with what framing.

e.g. "The CEO and his wife" frames women as appendages of men's roles

🌀

Stereotypical associations

Words consistently paired with one gender in training data, media, and public discourse — "aggressive leader" (male), "nurturing teacher" (female).

e.g. Describing a female politician's appearance before her policies

Framework

The four pillars
of detection

GenderLens analyses text across four distinct dimensions. Each catches a different form of bias. Together they give a complete picture.

Pronoun asymmetry · up to 30 pts

Pronoun Analysis

Counts every he/him/his vs she/her/hers vs they/them and measures the ratio for unnamed, generic referents — not named individuals. A 96–100% masculine rate is 30 points. Balanced (45–55%) is zero.

Biased "Every engineer must test his own code"
Neutral "Every engineer must test their own code"

Research by Gastil (1990) found that generic masculine pronouns lead readers to picture male subjects 88% of the time. This isn't a reading problem — it's a language design problem. When "he" is the default, women and non-binary people are linguistically erased from the subject of entire domains. Studies show this affects career aspirations, perceived competence, and sense of belonging.

Gendered language · up to 25 pts

Gendered Language

Flags role nouns with gender-neutral alternatives (chairman → chair), power-coded adjectives used asymmetrically (bossy applied to women, assertive to men), and compound words that use "man" generically.

Biased "Mankind's greatest achievement"
Neutral "Humanity's greatest achievement"

A 2022 University of Houston study found that masculine leadership titles like "chairman" reinforced stereotypes that associate men with leadership and actively undermined women's perceived suitability for those roles — even when controlling for actual position. The word itself, not the person holding it, does the cognitive work of exclusion.

Anthropomorphism · up to 20 pts

Anthropomorphism

Catches when non-human entities — ships, AI systems, robots, countries, storms, cars — are given gendered pronouns or names without justification. One ship instance is 1 pt. An AI system given female pronouns is 4 pts (servile female trope).

Flagged "She set sail on her maiden voyage"
Neutral "The ship set sail on its maiden voyage"

UNESCO's 2019 report "I'd Blush If I Could" documented how female-voiced, female-named AI assistants (Siri, Alexa, Cortana) that respond submissively to commands reinforce the association of women with service, obedience, and availability. Ships get a lower penalty because female ship naming is a very old nautical convention. AI being female is a design choice made in the 21st century.

Domain stereotyping · up to 25 pts

Domain Stereotyping

Identifies when entire subject domains implicitly assume a gendered actor. STEM, finance, leadership, and law default to male framing. Caregiving, nursing, teaching, and domestic topics default to female framing.

Biased "Ask your nurse — she'll explain"
Neutral "Ask your nurse — they'll explain"

Domain stereotyping is uniquely insidious because it creates a feedback loop. When tech articles consistently use "he" for engineers, fewer women see themselves as engineers. When fewer women are engineers, tech articles have fewer women to quote, so they keep using "he". The bias in language reflects the bias in the world, and then makes it worse. Breaking the loop requires actively choosing inclusive language even when it feels "unnatural".

History

How our understanding
has evolved

The recognition of gender bias in language is surprisingly recent — and the fight to change it is ongoing.

← Scroll to explore the timeline →
1553
The "generic he" is formalised
English grammarians officially codify "he" as the grammatically correct pronoun for unknown-gender subjects, a rule that would dominate for 400 years
1850
An Act of Parliament
The UK Interpretation Act legally declares that "he" includes "she" for legal purposes — officially erasing women from legal language while also supposedly including them
1975
Casey Miller & Kate Swift
Publication of "Words and Women" — the first major popular-press book to document systematic gender bias in English vocabulary and grammar
1980
Linguistic studies begin
Researchers begin formally documenting that "generic he" causes readers to mentally picture male subjects — proving the language has real cognitive effects
1993
AP Stylebook changes
The Associated Press Stylebook — the bible of US journalism — begins recommending gender-neutral alternatives to "chairman", "stewardess", and similar terms
2003
Word2Vec exposes AI bias
Early language models trained on internet text reveal that "man is to doctor as woman is to nurse" — AI doesn't create bias, it amplifies whatever bias is in the training data
2016
Bolukbasi et al. study
Landmark paper "Man is to Computer Programmer as Woman is to Homemaker?" formally documents occupational gender bias in word embeddings used by AI systems globally
2019
UNESCO: "I'd Blush If I Could"
UNESCO report documents how female-voiced, submissive AI assistants globally reinforce gender stereotypes — and calls for industry-wide redesign
2021
Gender Gap Tracker
Large-scale automated study using NLP across 7 news outlets confirms men are quoted 3× more than women — and that this gap has barely changed in a decade
2024
LLMs and occupational stereotypes
ACM research proves modern LLMs like GPT-4 express stronger occupational gender stereotypes than actual US Bureau of Labor statistics — AI amplifies bias beyond reality
Now
Detection tools emerge

AI & Technology

How AI amplifies
what it learns

AI doesn't invent gender bias — it inherits it from human-written text, then amplifies it at scale.

AI bias in action
Prompt
Complete this sentence: "The doctor examined the patient and told ..."
AI response (typical)
"...told him to rest for a week. He prescribed medication and asked him to follow up in ten days."

What the research shows

LLMs express occupational gender bias aligned with human stereotypes — and in some cases amplify them beyond real-world statistics. (ACM, 2023)
When completing sentences about professions, GPT-4 assigns male pronouns to doctors and engineers at rates higher than actual US Bureau of Labor workforce data
74% of voice AI assistants globally are assigned female voices for service/assistant roles — a design choice, not a default (UNESCO, 2019)
The classic word embedding: "Man is to computer programmer as woman is to homemaker" — Bolukbasi et al., 2016 — proved AI encodes occupational stereotypes
Fine-tuned models (like GPT-4) show reduced occupational bias vs raw pretrained models — proving deliberate debiasing works when prioritised
Training data overrepresents internet contributors — who skew younger, male, and Western — making AI outputs systematically biased in all three directions
Research

The science of
linguistic bias

These aren't opinions — they're documented, peer-reviewed findings about how biased language affects human cognition.

🧠

Generic "he" changes who you picture

Gastil (1990) found that when people read sentences with generic "he" (e.g. "the doctor… he"), they picture a male subject 88% of the time — even when told the gender is unknown. Replacing "he" with "they" drops this to roughly 50%.

J. Gastil, Social Psychology Quarterly, 1990
📋

Biased job listings reduce applications

Gaucher, Friesen & Kay (2011) showed that job listings with more masculine-coded words (competitive, dominant, ambitious) received significantly fewer applications from women — not because women were less qualified, but because the language signalled they weren't welcome.

Journal of Personality & Social Psychology, 2011
🏛️

Masculine titles undermine female leaders

University of Houston (2022) found that masculine leadership titles like "chairman" and "councilman" reinforce stereotypes linking men to leadership — actively undermining women's perceived authority in those roles, even when the woman holds the position.

University of Houston, Archer et al., 2022

Solutions

How to write
more inclusively

Small, specific changes make a real difference. Here's what to actually do.

For individuals & writers

1

Default to "they" for unknown gender

Singular they is grammatically correct, widely accepted, and requires no extra words. "Each employee should submit their form" — done.

2

Replace "man-" compound words

Mankind → humanity. Manpower → workforce. Man-hours → person-hours. These swaps lose nothing and gain clarity.

3

Check role nouns

Chairman → chair. Stewardess → flight attendant. Fireman → firefighter. Run a quick search in your document for gendered role nouns before publishing.

4

Notice adjective asymmetry

Would you use this adjective for a man in the same role? If "bossy" wouldn't appear in a description of a male CEO, don't use it for a female one.

For organisations & teams

5

Audit job listings before posting

Tools exist to measure masculine/feminine-coded language in job ads. Listings with more balanced language attract 42% more diverse applicant pools.

6

Update your style guide

Add a section on inclusive language with specific examples. Make it a standard, not a suggestion. Checklists work better than principles alone.

7

Measure, don't just intend

Use tools like GenderLens to analyse your published content. Intention without measurement doesn't create change. Set baselines and track progress.

8

Broaden who you quote

Actively seek female, non-binary, and underrepresented expert sources. Bias in sourcing creates bias in the final piece even when the prose itself is neutral.
