Why this matters

Gender bias in language is measurable — and more widespread than most people realise

9×
"He" pronouns appear roughly nine times more often than "she" in news media — even when the subject's gender is unknown.
Gustafsson Sendén et al., Sex Roles, 2015
3×
Men are quoted approximately three times as often as women in mainstream English-language news across politics, business and sport.
Gender Gap Tracker, PLOS ONE, 2021
83%
Wikipedia biographies are about men. Women account for just 17% of published biographies and female articles are more likely to be deleted.
Wikipedia gender bias research, 2021

The four bias types GenderLens detects

Pronoun asymmetry
When he/him/his dramatically outnumbers she/her/hers in contexts where gender isn't specified, or when objects are gendered by default.
e.g. "The engineer should review his code"
Gendered language
Words that embed a gender assumption — role nouns like "chairman", "mankind", "manpower", or adjectives applied asymmetrically.
e.g. "The chairman opened proceedings"
Anthropomorphism
Non-human entities given a gender without reason. Ships and countries are often called "she"; military hardware and computers "he".
e.g. "She set sail on her maiden voyage"
Domain stereotyping
Topics where the framing assumes a gendered actor. STEM and finance default to male; caregiving and education default to female.
e.g. "Nurses should remind their patients"
